Dengue fever is not a distant threat — it is a constant presence in Malaysian life. In 2024 alone, over 130,000 cases were reported nationwide, making it one of the worst years on record. Behind every number is a fever, a hospital bed, and a family waiting. Yet despite decades of awareness campaigns and neighbourhood gotong-royong clean-ups, the Aedes mosquito continues to thrive in our rapidly urbanising landscape.

The chart below shows how dengue has evolved from a manageable public health concern into a crisis that regularly exceeds 100,000 cases per year. Three moments stand out: the 2015 record outbreak, the unexpected suppression during COVID-19 lockdowns in 2021, and the dramatic 2024 resurgence.

02

It follows the rain

Dengue outbreaks are not random — they follow a predictable pattern tied to rainfall. When it rains, water collects in containers, drains, and construction sites, creating perfect breeding grounds for Aedes mosquitoes. The mosquitoes breed within 7–10 days, and symptoms appear 4–14 days after a bite. This creates a natural delay between rainfall and the rise in dengue cases.

The chart below compares annual rainfall in Selangor with national dengue cases for the years 2014–2018. While rainfall is one factor, the data also shows that other drivers — such as virus strain shifts and urbanisation — play an important role.

03

Selangor pays half the bill

Not all states suffer equally. Selangor, home to roughly 20% of Malaysia's population, consistently carries almost half of the nation's dengue burden. In 2015, Selangor alone reported over 63,000 cases — an incidence rate exceeding 1,000 per 100,000 residents. Why? A combination of rapid urbanisation, construction sites with standing water, and the highest population density in the country creates a perfect storm for Aedes mosquito breeding.

04

The hidden pattern

There are four strains of the dengue virus, labelled DENV-1 through DENV-4. When the dominant strain in circulation shifts, the population has limited immunity to the new variant, and cases explode. This is called a serotype shift.

The 2014–2015 record outbreak coincided with DENV-1 becoming the dominant circulating strain, replacing DENV-2 which had previously dominated. By 2018–2019, DENV-2 surged back again. Understanding this cycle is key to predicting when the next major epidemic will strike.

The heatmap below reveals another hidden pattern: dengue epidemics do not strike all states equally or simultaneously. This uneven timing suggests that local conditions — rainfall patterns, urbanisation levels, and immunity gaps — matter as much as national trends.

05

What comes next?

Malaysia's monsoon patterns are shifting. The northeast monsoon — which drives the dengue season — has been arriving earlier in recent years. If this trend continues, the dengue season will start earlier and last longer, stretching already-thin public health resources.

The chart below shows how dengue cases are distributed across months for each state in 2010. Every state has a different seasonal peak, which means a single national control strategy cannot address all states equally.
