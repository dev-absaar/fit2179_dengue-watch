Dengue fever is not a distant threat — it is a constant presence in Malaysian life. In 2024 alone, over 130,000 cases were reported nationwide, making it one of the worst years on record. Behind every number is a fever, a hospital bed, and a family waiting. Yet despite decades of awareness campaigns and neighbourhood gotong-royong clean-ups, the Aedes mosquito continues to thrive in our rapidly urbanising landscape.

The chart below shows how dengue has evolved from a manageable public health concern into a crisis that regularly exceeds 100,000 cases per year. Three moments stand out: the 2015 record outbreak, the unexpected suppression during COVID-19 lockdowns, and the dramatic 2024 resurgence.

02

It follows the rain

Dengue outbreaks are not random — they follow a predictable pattern tied to rainfall. When it rains, water collects in containers, drains, and construction sites, creating perfect breeding grounds for Aedes mosquitoes. The mosquitoes breed within 7–10 days, and symptoms appear 4–14 days after a bite.

The result is a 2–4 week lag between heavy rainfall and dengue spikes. The chart below overlays weekly rainfall in Selangor with national dengue cases. Watch how the blue rainfall peaks consistently lead the orange case peaks by several weeks.

03

Selangor pays half the bill

Not all states suffer equally. Selangor, home to roughly 20% of Malaysia's population, consistently carries almost half of the nation's dengue burden. In 2015, Selangor alone reported over 63,000 cases — an incidence rate exceeding 1,000 per 100,000 residents. Why? A combination of rapid urbanisation, construction sites with standing water, and the highest population density in the country creates a perfect storm for Aedes mosquito breeding.

04

The hidden pattern

There are four strains of the dengue virus, labelled DENV-1 through DENV-4. When the dominant strain in circulation shifts, the population has limited immunity to the new variant, and cases explode. This is called a serotype shift.

The 2015 record outbreak was not simply caused by more mosquitoes or more rain — it coincided with a dramatic shift from DENV-1 to DENV-2 as the dominant circulating strain. Understanding this hidden cycle is the key to predicting when the next major epidemic will strike.

05

What comes next?

Malaysia's monsoon patterns are shifting. The northeast monsoon — which drives the dengue season — has been arriving earlier in recent years. If this trend continues, the dengue season will start earlier and last longer, stretching already-thin public health resources.

The charts below compare the monthly shape of dengue outbreaks across three eras. Notice how the peak appears to shift and broaden over time — a warning sign that climate change may be reshaping Malaysia's dengue landscape.
